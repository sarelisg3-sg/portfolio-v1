## The problem

In Mexico City, parking meters are managed by **different apps depending on the
zone**. A driver moving through the city needs several apps, each with its own
signup, interface and payment method — causing confusion, wasted time and
**avoidable fines**.

Analysis of existing apps (Blinkay, Parkum, Mueve Ciudad) revealed recurring
complaints: GPS failures, schedule sync errors, balance top-up problems and
complicated interfaces.

## Target user

The project initially focused on the Miguel Hidalgo borough — the area with the
most parking meters in the city, with an estimated usage above **5 million
sessions per year**.

The primary profile: **Daniela, 34, an architect** and frequent driver. She needs
to manage parking centrally because traffic chaos and juggling multiple apps take
time away from her work.

## Information architecture

The app's structure was defined through **card sorting**: similarity matrix,
dendrograms (Actual Agreement Method) and a standardization grid. The conclusions
guided the design:

1. Parking-flow functions (time, rates, payment, extension) are perceived as one
   coherent group — they must live together.
2. Administrative functions (account, payment methods, vehicle) form separate
   sections.
3. The most frequent actions (geolocation, park, payment) must be reachable from
   the first screen.

## The solution (MVP)

An app that detects the parking meter via **automatic geolocation**, shows rates
and schedules, lets the user **select parking time** and **pays automatically**
with the registered card — across every zone in the city.

## Validation

The high-fidelity Figma prototype was evaluated with a **heuristic evaluation**
(Nielsen) and **usability tests with 5 users** on a real scenario: park at meter
A248, pay for 1 hour, check remaining time, extend by 30 minutes and review
payment history.

- **127 seconds** on average to complete all 5 tasks
- **4 of 5 tasks** at 100% success (the fifth: 80%)
- **4.2/5** average satisfaction

Findings led to improvements in key-button visibility, text size and system
feedback.

## From prototype to code

The complete prototype — all ~39 screens, including the 4-step onboarding — is
rebuilt as a **real interactive Next.js app**, presented in an iPhone frame like
the original prototype. The parking session runs a **live countdown**, the total
is computed in real time from the rate ($3.50 / 15 min), and heuristic-evaluation
findings were addressed in code (e.g. error prevention: you cannot confirm
without selecting a time).

Design tokens and components follow the architecture of my
[personal design system](https://design-system-sare1.vercel.app), with the brand
scale recalculated to ParkZone's teal.
